AI Is Destroying Software Valuations. Here's Why That's Great News for Your Business.

Jean Louis Hardy | 2026.03.27 | 10 min read

There is a chart making the rounds on Wall Street right now that should make every service business owner feel very, very good about their life choices.

For the first time in recorded market history, the price-to-earnings ratio of publicly traded SaaS companies has fallen below the S&P 500 average. SaaS P/E multiples now sit at roughly 22.7x - down from a peak of 84x during the 2021 pandemic bubble. The entire software industry, the sector that was supposed to eat every other sector alive, is getting repriced. Downward. Fast.

Meanwhile, private equity firms are deploying record capital into service businesses - HVAC companies, plumbing operations, roofing contractors, electrical firms, pest control. The "boring" businesses that software engineers used to joke about. The businesses that your neighbor does not understand when you try to explain what you do at a dinner party.

Those businesses are now the smart money bet. And the reason is the same technology that is destroying software valuations: artificial intelligence.

The Great SaaS Repricing

To understand why this matters for your business, you need to understand what happened to software.

The SaaS business model was built on a simple premise: charge per seat, per month, forever. Every employee at a company needed a Salesforce license, a Slack account, a Zoom subscription, a project management tool. The more employees a company hired, the more software revenue grew. It was a beautiful flywheel - and Wall Street priced it accordingly.

At the 2021 peak, investors valued SaaS companies at 84 times earnings. For context, the historical average for the S&P 500 is roughly 20 to 22x. Software was trading at four times the market average because investors believed the per-seat model would compound forever.

Then AI showed up. And it broke the model.

The SaaS Multiple Collapse

SaaS P/E multiples have compressed from 84x (2021 peak) to approximately 22.7x in early 2026 - falling below the S&P 500 average for the first time ever. The per-seat software model that drove decades of growth is being fundamentally disrupted by AI that replaces the seats themselves.

AI Is Eating the Per-Seat Model

The per-seat pricing model only works when companies keep adding seats. When AI starts replacing the humans sitting in those seats, the entire revenue model collapses.

Look at what has happened in the past 18 months:

Klarna - the buy-now-pay-later giant - announced that its AI assistant was doing the work of 700 full-time customer service agents. Seven hundred seats eliminated. That is not a reduction in headcount growth. That is an elimination of existing headcount. Every software vendor that charged Klarna per-seat for customer service tools, workforce management platforms, and communication systems lost revenue overnight. Klarna's CEO Sebastian Siemiatkowski publicly stated that the company had stopped hiring entirely and was letting natural attrition shrink the workforce while AI absorbed the work.

Salesforce - the company that practically invented the per-seat SaaS model - froze hiring across the company. Marc Benioff told investors that AI agents could handle tasks that previously required human employees, and that Salesforce would prioritize AI deployment over headcount expansion. Think about that. The company that built its $30 billion revenue base on selling seats is now telling the market that companies will need fewer seats. Salesforce is essentially admitting that its own technology is cannibalizing its core business model.

UiPath - the robotic process automation leader that was once valued at $35 billion - has seen its market cap crater to under $8 billion. The company built its business on automating repetitive tasks inside enterprises. Then generative AI arrived and started automating those same tasks without the complex implementation UiPath required. Customers started asking why they needed expensive RPA licenses when a large language model could handle the same workflows at a fraction of the cost. Co-founder Daniel Dines stepped back as CEO as the company struggled to redefine its value proposition in an AI-native world.

These are not edge cases. These are category leaders. And they are all telling the same story: AI is reducing the number of humans that companies need, which reduces the number of software seats they buy, which reduces software revenue, which compresses software valuations.

The Software Valuation Death Spiral

AI replaces human workers at customer companies
Fewer workers = fewer software seats purchased
Per-seat revenue contracts instead of expanding
Net revenue retention drops below 100%
Growth narratives collapse
Result: SaaS P/E multiples compress from 84x to 22.7x

Why Service Businesses Are Moving in the Opposite Direction

Here is the part that should make you sit up straight in your chair.

While software valuations are compressing, service business valuations are expanding. Private equity firms - the most sophisticated buyers in the world, the people who spend millions on due diligence before writing a check - are actively shifting capital away from software and into services.

The reason is simple: AI cannot do what you do.

AI can replace a customer service agent sitting at a desk typing responses. It cannot replace a technician crawling under a house to fix a burst pipe at 2 AM. AI can automate a sales development representative's email outreach. It cannot rewire an electrical panel to pass code inspection. AI can generate marketing copy and analyze spreadsheets. It cannot climb onto a roof in August heat to replace damaged shingles.

Your business has a moat. A physical, tangible, irreplaceable moat. The work requires human hands, human judgment in unpredictable physical environments, and human relationships built on trust over years of service. No large language model is going to eliminate that. Not in five years. Not in ten. Probably not ever.

And the smart money knows it.

The PE Flood Into "Boring" Businesses

Blackstone - the world's largest alternative asset manager with over $1 trillion in assets under management - acquired Champions Group in a deal valuing the residential HVAC and plumbing platform at approximately $3.2 billion. Champions Group is exactly the kind of business that a software founder would have dismissed as "boring" five years ago. It sends technicians to people's homes to fix air conditioners and unclog drains. Blackstone looked at that business and saw a fortress.

Why? Because Champions Group has recurring maintenance contracts. Customers call back every year. The service requires licensed technicians who show up physically. The demand is non-discretionary - you cannot choose to skip air conditioning in Houston or plumbing in Chicago. And AI cannot disintermediate the delivery model. Every single revenue driver is AI-resistant in ways that software revenue is not.

Vertex Roofing and similar platform plays in the roofing vertical have attracted significant PE interest as well. Roofing companies operate with a unique combination of characteristics that PE firms love: recurring storm damage cycles, insurance-funded revenue streams, high barriers to entry through licensing and bonding requirements, and a labor force that cannot be replaced by technology. You need humans on roofs. Period.

The broader numbers tell the story. According to PitchBook data, PE deal volume in residential and commercial services has increased over 40 percent since 2023, even as overall PE deal activity has remained flat. The capital is rotating. It is moving away from sectors vulnerable to AI disruption and toward sectors that AI makes more efficient without making obsolete. We covered this shift in depth in our analysis of why PE firms are coming for your industry.

That distinction is everything.

The Key Distinction

Software companies are vulnerable to AI because AI replaces their customers' need for the product. Service businesses benefit from AI because AI makes their operations more efficient without replacing the core service delivery. AI helps you run a better plumbing company. It does not replace the plumber.

The Numbers Tell the Story

Let me put real numbers on this shift so you can see it clearly.

In 2021, the average publicly traded SaaS company traded at roughly 15x to 20x revenue. Today, many trade at 4x to 8x revenue. Companies like Zoom went from a market cap of $160 billion to under $20 billion. DocuSign collapsed from $50 billion to roughly $12 billion. The software sector has shed trillions in combined market value.

Meanwhile, EBITDA multiples for well-run service businesses in the $5M to $50M revenue range have moved from a historical average of 4x to 5x up to 5.2x to 7.8x - and in some cases higher for businesses with strong recurring revenue, documented systems, and AI-enhanced operations.

Valuation Shift: Software vs. Services

SaaS Revenue Multiple (2021): 15-20x
SaaS Revenue Multiple (2026): 4-8x
Compression: -60% to -73%
 
Service Business EBITDA Multiple (2021): 4-5x
Service Business EBITDA Multiple (2026): 5.2-7.8x

The gap is closing - and for the first time, it favors services

For a service business with $2M in EBITDA, the difference between a 4.5x and a 7x multiple is $5 million in exit value. That is not a rounding error. That is a life-changing number. And the market is moving in your direction.

Why Human-Touch Businesses Command Premiums in an AI World

There are four structural reasons why service businesses become more valuable as AI proliferates. Understanding these reasons is important because they are not temporary - they are accelerating.

1. Non-Discretionary Demand. When your air conditioner breaks in July, you call someone. When your sewer line backs up, you call someone. When your roof leaks during a storm, you call someone. This demand does not compress during recessions, does not get automated by AI, and does not get replaced by a cheaper alternative. Software subscriptions are discretionary - companies cut seats during downturns. Emergency plumbing is not discretionary. That predictability is worth a premium to buyers.

2. Physical Delivery Moat. Every dollar of revenue in your business requires a human being to physically travel to a location and perform work with their hands. That constraint, which you have probably cursed on days when you cannot find enough technicians, is actually your greatest competitive advantage. It creates a barrier to entry that no amount of venture capital or AI can overcome. A startup with $100 million in funding cannot disrupt your plumbing business the way Uber disrupted taxis, because the service itself requires licensed, skilled labor performing physical work in unpredictable environments.

3. Relationship-Based Recurring Revenue. Your maintenance contracts and service agreements are built on trust. Mrs. Johnson calls your company every year for her furnace inspection because your technician Dave has been coming to her house for six years and she trusts him. That relationship is not digitizable. It does not exist in a database that a competitor can replicate. It lives in the handshake, the follow-up call, the time Dave noticed her water heater looked like it was failing and saved her from a $15,000 basement flood. That is a moat that compounds over time and only gets stronger.

4. AI Enhances Rather Than Replaces. This is the critical point. AI does not threaten your business - it makes it better. AI scheduling reduces drive time between jobs. AI customer communication captures after-hours leads that used to go to voicemail. AI financial analysis gives you real-time visibility into margins and cash flow. AI predictive maintenance helps you sell higher-margin service contracts. Every dollar you invest in AI makes your existing business more efficient and more valuable. In software, AI replaces the product. In services, AI enhances it.

What Smart Money Sees That Everyone Else Misses

When a PE firm like Blackstone deploys $3.2 billion into a residential services platform, they are not making a charity bet. They have modeled every scenario. They have stress-tested the thesis against AI disruption, economic downturns, labor shortages, and regulatory changes. And they still wrote the check.

What they see is a sector with durable competitive advantages that are strengthening, not weakening, as technology advances. They see businesses where AI adoption improves margins by 15 to 25 percent without replacing the core revenue engine. They see recurring revenue backed by non-discretionary demand. They see fragmented markets with thousands of potential acquisition targets - businesses like yours - that can be rolled up into larger platforms at attractive multiples.

They also see the flip side: software companies whose revenue models are actively being cannibalized by the very technology they sell. Enterprise customers using AI to reduce headcount, which reduces seat-based software spending, which reduces growth rates, which compresses multiples. It is a structural decline, not a cyclical one. The per-seat model is not coming back.

The capital is moving from sectors where AI destroys value to sectors where AI creates value. Your sector is on the receiving end.

Your "Boring" Business Is the Smart Money Bet

I know what some of you are thinking. You have spent years listening to people at conferences talk about "disruption" and "digital transformation" and "the future of work" - and none of it ever seemed relevant to your HVAC company or your plumbing operation. You sent technicians out in trucks, fixed things that broke, and collected payment. Simple. Boring. Not exactly Silicon Valley material.

Here is what I need you to understand: that simplicity is now your greatest asset.

The same people who called your business "boring" are now watching their software portfolios get repriced downward while your business value increases. The venture capitalists who would not have returned your call in 2021 are now scrambling to get into your sector. The PE firms that spent the last decade overpaying for SaaS companies are now writing bigger checks for service businesses than they ever wrote for software.

The market has figured out something that you have known intuitively for decades: businesses that require skilled humans to physically solve real-world problems for other humans are extraordinarily durable. They survive recessions, survive technology shifts, survive everything - because the demand is permanent and the delivery cannot be automated.

You are not behind. You are ahead. The market is just now catching up to where you already are.

What This Means for Your Exit

If you are a service business owner in the $5M to $50M revenue range who has been thinking about an exit in the next three to five years, the current market dynamics are the most favorable they have been in at least a decade.

PE dry powder - the capital committed but not yet deployed - sits at $2.6 trillion globally. A significant and growing share of that capital is targeted at service businesses. Buyers are competing for quality targets, which pushes multiples higher. And the AI-readiness premium we have written about previously is amplifying the effect: service businesses that have adopted AI tools and can document the operational improvements are commanding an additional 0.3x to 0.5x on their EBITDA multiple.

The combination of sector-level tailwinds (capital rotating into services) plus company-level advantages (AI-driven efficiency gains) creates a window where well-prepared service businesses can achieve exit valuations that would have been unthinkable five years ago. The software founders are getting repriced downward. You are getting repriced upward. That is not a coincidence. That is the market telling you exactly how valuable your business is in a world where AI changes everything but the need for skilled humans to show up and do the work.

Your "boring" business is the best investment thesis in the market right now. It is time to make sure you are positioned to capture what it is worth.
